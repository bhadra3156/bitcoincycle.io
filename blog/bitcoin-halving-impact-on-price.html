Bitcoin Halving and Its Impact on Bitcoin Price

BitcoinCycle.io · May 2025 · 8 min read

Every four years, something remarkable happens inside the Bitcoin network: the reward miners receive for validating transactions is cut in half. This event — known as the halving — is one of the most anticipated events in all of crypto. But what does it actually mean for price? And does it explain why Bitcoin moves in such dramatic, repeatable cycles?

This is the question at the heart of everything BitcoinCycle.io is built on. Let's go deep.

What Is the Bitcoin Halving?

Bitcoin was designed by Satoshi Nakamoto with a fixed supply cap of 21 million BTC. New coins enter circulation only through mining — a process where computers compete to solve cryptographic puzzles and add blocks to the blockchain. The winner receives a block reward in freshly minted BTC.

Satoshi hard-coded a deflationary rule into Bitcoin's protocol: every 210,000 blocks (approximately every 4 years), the block reward is cut in half. This continues until all 21 million Bitcoin have been mined — estimated around the year 2140.

The Supply Shock Mechanism

To understand why halvings affect price, you need to apply basic supply and demand economics to a fixed-supply asset.

Before the halving

At 6.25 BTC per block with a ~10-minute block time, miners produce approximately 900 new BTC per day. These coins flow continuously onto exchanges as miners sell to cover operational costs — electricity, hardware, staff. This creates significant, predictable daily sell pressure on the market.

After the halving

That daily supply drops to 450 BTC per day — overnight. At $63,000 per BTC, this represents roughly $28 million in daily sell pressure disappearing from the market. If demand stays constant, or continues to grow, the reduced supply creates sustained upward price pressure. This is the supply shock.

💡 Think of it this way: Imagine a gold mine that produces 1,000 ounces per day suddenly starts producing 500 ounces per day. If investors, jewellers, and central banks still want the same amount of gold, the price must rise to ration the reduced supply. Bitcoin works the same way — but the halving is scheduled, predictable, and written in immutable code.

Why the Cycle Takes 12–18 Months to Play Out

One of the most common misconceptions is that halvings cause immediate price spikes. They don't — and understanding why is crucial to using cycle analysis effectively.

The supply shock is real, but it takes time to propagate through the market. Here's the typical sequence every cycle follows:

Months 1–3 post-halving: Consolidation

The market has largely anticipated the halving. Short-term traders who bought the rumour sell the news. Price often consolidates or dips modestly right after. This phase shakes out weak hands and sets the foundation for the real move.

Months 3–9: Quiet accumulation

The cumulative effect of reduced issuance begins to tighten available supply on exchanges. On-chain metrics — exchange outflows, long-term holder accumulation, MVRV ratios — begin flashing conviction signals. Institutional players accumulate quietly.

Months 9–18: Price discovery

Media coverage accelerates. New retail participants enter for the first time. Institutional demand grows. Supply on exchanges tightens sharply. This is where the explosive, parabolic price moves occur — the phase most people think of as "the bull market."

Post-ATH: Compression phase

Overextension, euphoria, and cascading profit-taking trigger a sharp correction. In the 1064/364 model used here at BitcoinCycle.io, this compression phase lasts exactly 364 days — from ATH to the following ATL, resetting the cycle for the next expansion.

What the 2024 Halving Means for This Cycle

The fourth halving occurred on 19 April 2024 at block height 840,000, reducing rewards to 3.125 BTC per block. Bitcoin was trading near $63,000 at the time — already close to its previous all-time high. This is unprecedented heading into a halving.

Several factors make this cycle structurally different from all previous ones:

Spot Bitcoin ETFs

For the first time in Bitcoin's history, institutional and retail capital can flow into Bitcoin through regulated ETF vehicles. BlackRock's IBIT, Fidelity's FBTC, and others accumulated tens of billions in assets within months of their January 2024 launch. This adds a powerful, persistent new demand driver that previous halvings simply did not have.

Miner maturity and reduced sell pressure

With rewards at 3.125 BTC, only the most capital-efficient mining operations can survive profitably. Well-capitalised public miners like Marathon Digital and CleanSpark hold rather than sell. This further reduces the daily sell pressure that Bitcoin markets must absorb.

Macro environment

Central bank monetary policy, inflation dynamics, and the global search for non-sovereign stores of value all feed into Bitcoin demand. With sovereign debt levels at historic highs globally, Bitcoin's fixed supply narrative has never been more relevant to institutional allocators.

⚠️ Diminishing but still significant returns: While +9,500% cycles are history, even a move from $63k to $150,000–$200,000 would represent +140% to +200% — extraordinary performance by any traditional asset class standard.

Common Halving Myths, Debunked

"The halving is already priced in"

This argument has been made before every single halving — and has been wrong every time. Markets are not perfectly efficient, especially in crypto where retail sentiment, leverage cycles, and liquidity constraints create persistent and measurable mispricings.

"This cycle will be different (bearish)"

Every cycle feels different from the inside. Macro conditions shift, regulation evolves, new narratives emerge. But the underlying supply-demand mechanics of a provably scarce asset with growing adoption have reasserted themselves across 15 years and four complete cycles.

"Miners don't matter anymore"

Transaction fees are growing as a proportion of miner revenue — which is by design. But at current fee levels, block rewards still dominate miner income. Miner sell pressure remains a significant, measurable supply variable tracked by on-chain analysts globally.

Conclusion

Bitcoin's halving is not a magic price catalyst — it is a structural supply event engineered into the protocol that systematically reduces new issuance every four years. Combined with growing global adoption, maturing institutional infrastructure, and the psychological impact of each cycle on market participants, it has produced some of the most dramatic, repeatable price appreciation in financial history.
