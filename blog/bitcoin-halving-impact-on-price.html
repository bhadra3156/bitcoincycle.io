Bitcoin Halving and Its Impact on Bitcoin Price

By BitcoinCycle.io  ·  May 2025  ·  8 min read

Every four years, something remarkable happens inside the Bitcoin network: the reward miners receive for validating transactions is cut in half. This event — known as the halving — is one of the most anticipated and most discussed events in all of crypto. But what does it actually mean for price? And does it explain why Bitcoin moves in such dramatic, repeatable cycles?

This is the question at the heart of everything BitcoinCycle.io is built on. Let's go deep.

What Is the Bitcoin Halving?

Bitcoin was designed with a fixed supply cap of 21 million BTC. New coins enter circulation only through mining — a process where computers compete to solve cryptographic puzzles and add blocks to the blockchain. The winner receives a block reward in fresh BTC.

Satoshi Nakamoto hard-coded a rule into Bitcoin's protocol: every 210,000 blocks (~4 years), this reward is cut in half. The halvings proceed like clockwork:

The pattern is clear: each halving has historically been followed by a significant bull market. Returns diminish over time as Bitcoin's market cap grows, but the directional signal has been consistent across every cycle.

The Supply Shock Mechanism

To understand why halvings affect price, you need to understand basic supply and demand economics applied to a fixed-supply asset.

Before the halving

Miners produce approximately 900 new BTC per day (at 6.25 BTC per block). These coins flow directly onto exchanges as miners sell to cover operational costs — electricity, hardware, staff. This creates consistent daily sell pressure.

After the halving

That daily supply drops to 450 BTC per day. Overnight, roughly $28 million (at $63k/BTC) in daily sell pressure disappears from the market. If demand stays constant — or grows — the reduced supply creates upward price pressure. This is the supply shock.

💡 Think of it like this: Imagine a gold mine that produces 1,000 oz/day suddenly starts producing 500 oz/day. If jewellers, investors and central banks still want the same amount of gold, the price must rise to ration the reduced supply.

Why the Cycle Takes 12–18 Months to Play Out

One of the most common misconceptions is that halvings cause immediate price spikes. They don't — and that's actually important to understand.

The supply shock is real, but it takes time to work through the system. Here's the typical sequence:

Months 1–3: The market has largely priced in the halving in advance. Price often dips or consolidates right after as short-term traders sell the news.

Months 3–9: The cumulative effect of reduced issuance begins to tighten available supply. Long-term holders accumulate. On-chain metrics like the MVRV ratio and exchange outflows start signalling conviction.

Months 9–18: Media attention grows. New retail participants enter. Institutional interest picks up. The price discovery phase begins — this is where the explosive, parabolic moves occur.

Post-peak: Overextension, euphoria, and profit-taking trigger a sharp correction — the compression phase. In the 1064/364 model, this phase lasts exactly 364 days.

What Does the 2024 Halving Mean for Price?

The fourth halving occurred in April 2024 at a block height of 840,000, reducing rewards to 3.125 BTC per block. At the time, Bitcoin was trading around $63,000 — already near its previous all-time high, an unprecedented position heading into a halving.

This cycle has several unique characteristics:

Spot Bitcoin ETFs: For the first time, institutional capital can flow into Bitcoin through regulated ETF vehicles. BlackRock's IBIT alone accumulated over $17 billion in assets within months of launch. This adds a powerful new demand driver that previous halvings didn't have.

Reduced miner sell pressure: With rewards at 3.125 BTC, miners must be more capital efficient than ever. Well-capitalised operations will hold rather than sell. Weaker miners will exit, tightening supply further.

Diminishing but still significant returns: While +690% cycles are unlikely to repeat, Bitcoin moving from $63k to $150k–$200k would represent +140% to +200% — extraordinary by any asset class standard.

Common Halving Myths, Debunked

"The halving is already priced in"

This argument has been made before every halving — and has been wrong every time. Markets are not perfectly efficient, especially in crypto where retail sentiment, leverage cycles, and liquidity constraints create persistent mispricings.

"This cycle will be different"

Every cycle feels different from the inside. Macro conditions change, regulation shifts, new narratives emerge. But the underlying supply-demand mechanics of a deflationary asset with growing adoption remain consistent.

"Miners don't matter anymore"

Transaction fees are growing as a proportion of miner revenue, but block rewards still dominate at current fee levels. Miner sell pressure remains a significant and measurable supply variable.

Conclusion

Bitcoin's halving is not a magic price catalyst — it's a structural supply event built into the protocol that systematically reduces new issuance every four years. Combined with growing global demand, institutional adoption, and the psychological impact of each cycle on market participants, it has produced some of the most dramatic price appreciation in financial history.
